Markets for Traders

Most people encounter markets through prices. Traders encounter them through constraints. What can be traded, in what size, under what funding, through which channels, and at what cost — these questions determine outcomes long before any notion of “edge” becomes relevant.

Markets for Traders is built around a simple but often neglected premise: markets are not prediction problems. They are operational systems. Prices are the visible surface of a deeper machinery composed of liquidity provision, balance-sheet constraints, execution frictions, regulatory limits, and regime-dependent behavior.

This pillar exists to retrain intuition. It teaches readers to stop asking “Is this signal good?” and start asking “Can this position survive?” Survival, not optimality, is the dominant objective once markets are treated honestly.

The collection is deliberately explicit about its limits. It does not promise alpha. It does not offer deployable strategies. Instead, it builds the intellectual and operational scaffolding required before any professional trading activity can be responsibly attempted.

The Two-Book Architecture

The sequence is structured as apprenticeship rather than instruction. The first book establishes the grammar of market mechanics. The second forces the reader to operate within that grammar under stress. Neither stands alone.

Book I — The ABC of Trading

A conceptual foundation written for practitioners. Liquidity, carry, volatility, correlation, credit, and funding are introduced not as indicators but as economic mechanisms. The goal is literacy: understanding what market objects mean, how they interact, and why naive backtests so often mislead.

Book II — Markets for Traders

Ten mechanism-first laboratories implemented in Google Colab. Strategies operate on explicit curves, surfaces, and balance-sheet objects. Costs accumulate. Constraints bind. Regimes change. Failure is frequent — and instructive.

Important: These books are intentionally repetitive. Core ideas reappear across assets and mechanisms because real trading failure is rarely novel — it is structural.

The Execution Laboratories

The notebooks are not demonstrations and not templates. They are controlled environments designed to expose fragility. Each laboratory forces the reader to observe how a seemingly reasonable position becomes untenable once execution, leverage, or regime shifts are introduced.

The pedagogical objective is not success but calibration: understanding where models break, where intuition fails, and why institutional risk management dominates realized outcomes.

It repeatedly acknowledges the gap between simplified models and real markets and treats that gap as the central educational object rather than an inconvenience.
